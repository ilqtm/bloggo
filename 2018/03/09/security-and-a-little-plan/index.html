Logs, logs, logs.

I don’t have a clear idea of what to write about as far as tech goes, but I need to put something down regardless. A summation of the last two or three weeks and current plans. I’ve been getting a lot done from my living to do lists and am happy about it.

My focus shifted from development to a topic I didn’t realize I’d be able to get a foothold in: security. It makes sense though. I studied and was always excited about cryptography and the discrete math portions of my computer science curriculum in school. Good ol’ Bob and Alice (damn you Eve!). Not to mention a couple books I’ve read of my own volition.

  • The Code Book
  • Future Crimes

A large catalyst for this subtle shift (I’m still very much interested in cloud architecture and DevOps) was an unexpected in-person interview with a small SecOps unit. They also need a cloud guy, so, here’s hoping getting AWS certified was worth it as far as the job market is concerned. In addition, it’s inspired me to become Sec + certified as well, so I’ve been hitting the books and will be scheduling the exam soon.

They’re better than bad, they’re good.

Logs + GuardDuty, Splunk, or ELK (Elastic) Stack. Seattle started as a logging town and it still is, but these days it’s more likely to be data than wood. AWS (well, any place) produce an immense amount of logging data that can be collected and organized w/ both native services or imported software. VPC Flow logs, GuardDuty, Shield, CouldTrail, and and CloudWatch are most of the native ones. Integrated non AWS services are available for use w/ the AWS services or just on the infrastructure.

VPC Flow Logs and CloudTrail can be configured w/ SMS to shoot out alerts if permissions in policies are changed etc.(as long as they’re being tracked), those messages can trigger Lambda functions to revert those permissions so you don’t get leaky buckets or sprawl when the ACL’s become lax or too complicated to manually maintain (I saw this on the re:Invent 2017 footage). Or… really whatever you want to automate. I’ve got my AWS SA-A certification, but I’m still new to a lot of this, so it’s been exciting discovering all these fun possibilities.

However, since I’m becoming more security focused, this is all very useful for me to set up monitoring in a way that doesn’t require me to be an expert in reading any specific type of log. I figure most folks in IT could look at a log and determine its content, but having to constantly figure out new log types from different tools and multiplying that ad infinitem… then the task becomes daunting, nigh impossible. A way through this is with a clever toolbox called Elastic Stack (nee ELK stack). It democratizes log expertise by translating the original logs into a uniform format. Now you’re the expert! You can also use the K in the tech stack, Kibana, to build a dashboard to view the data from any angle to leverage what humans are best at, pattern recognition. Kibana stands on top of the E, Elasticsearch, a full text search (not just for logs!) that looks through the now uniform logs that Logstash translated.

Great! Now instead of spending time deciphering log formats, I can spend it looking at much larger sets w/ a question in mind or just to discover baseline behavior and topologies so anomalies or uncommon patterns can be recognized or better understood. This means better attack detection, control over the environment, and informative visualizations of whatever I might be curious of my logs about.

Splunk and GuardDuty have a similar, but what looks like slightly less flexible search w/ the advent of machine learning. The ML aspect makes determining my baseline behavior a little easier and can compare my topologies to well documented example shapes to help me understand what I’m looking at from any perspective (request locations, bandwidth, data served, what could be attacks, etc).

It’s big, it’s heavy, it’s wood (data)!

I’m excited to use these (new to me) tools to ensure the safety and efficacy of both personal creative projects and those needing monitoring for work. Learning has ever been worth the effort for me, I’m stoked to step into security in earnest and to have a bash at another exam.

Adios.
