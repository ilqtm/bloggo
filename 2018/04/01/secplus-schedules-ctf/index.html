CTF is so much different these days.

I bumped into my favorite professor of math and computer science from my alma mater a couple of weeks ago, Richard Weiss. It seemed like such a coincidence as I had recntly mentioned him in a job interview as an inspirational figure. I explained my current interests and goals and he suggested I join him and some current students for their weekly hacker club on campus.

I thought: this is the perfect way to sharpen and learn skills, to gain the perspective of the attacker, to defend against others, and to collaborate with a diverse group in skillset and background.
I showed up to the meeting 5 minutes early with notepad, pen, laptop, coffee, and nostalgia for the familiar CS Lab space.
I noticed the hacker club website posters on the wall and looked it up to see more of what I was in for. My old professor kindly showed up to tell me he couldn’t make it due to a meeting, but gave me some resources that have had me well enthralled since (more on this next). One other hopeful member, it was his first meeting too, arrived 20 minutes after start time. We chatted about our interests in the club, what we hope to get out of it, and our current projects both on and offline. He’s futher convinced me that python is a good path, so I’m using it more frequently. Good experience, but I’m definitely looking forward to a meeting that has at least one established member and isn’t during evaluations week.

Weiss’ Suggestions

The resources my old mentor mentioned are a few digital CTF challenge environments and premises. Overthewire.org, one the hacker club uses specifically, and some others for highschool students.
Any time I might have been spending reading for pleasure or watching something mindless has been spent trying to solve these puzzles.
The study reading and lynda.com course I’m watching to prepare for the Comptia Security + exam are helping with the challenges and hammering home a lot of things you feel are obvious DO NOT DO’s, but then you exploit those vulnerabilities via the challenge environment and they really get set in stone. They’re super fun and are doing a good job getting me to think creatively and to expand my administration and operations knowledge.

Goals

I’m going to:

  • post more
  • implement CloudFront
  • python more for my AWS resources
  • update with info on my freelance devops/admin/automating efforts
  • actually edit this template a bit for off-tech-topic stuff
  • it’s possible, probable, that I’ll be using an anti-charity platform like stickK to commit to these

More to come sooner than it has.
